About Me

There are a few titles that are indicative of the work I've completed; electrical engineer, software engineer, bioinformatician, but I think its most appropriate to characterize myself as an R&D engineer. I tend to take a systems level approach to design and strive to balance the most intimate design details while remaining cognizant of the 30,000 ft. big picture perspective. I have participated in every aspect of the development process, from prototype to pilot, basic research through advanced development. My enthusiasm is perpetuated by an unwavering demand for improvement via the application of math and science and I am optimistic about the next problem at hand.

For the past several years my attention has been focused on the development of novel analytics tools within the biological sciences domain. Typical solutions generally include one or more of the following: processing sequenced genetic information, alignment, phylogenetic tree construction, classification, clustering/binning, taxonomy assignment, ordination, dimensionality reduction, data visualization, and genomic feature selection. Due to the highly interdisciplinary nature of this work I have collaborated alongside biologists to provide actionable strategy with regard to enormous datasets that are often characterized by noise and unlabeled data. From the programatic side, I have relied predominately on Matlab and Python for their rapid prototyping capabilities as well as a suite of application specific software tools.

Previously, I assumed a more classical electrical engineering role working as a lead engineer in a multinational R&D group developing a highly-scalable wireless electronic paper retail signage system. The resulting prototype inspired numerous patents and two successful long-term pilot demonstrations with partners both in academia and industry. Other notable projects I have worked on include a refined high frequency modulation circuit to reduce coherent noise in laser illumination applications (patented) and several RFID antennas for handheld barcode scanners. As a prelude to my engineering career, I flew with the FAA in a hollowed-out Convair 440 performing data acquisition on a novel GPS-based aircraft landing system.
